Case Study: Guaranteed Pickup

Building from a Blank Page: Launching Guaranteed Pickup, a First-of-Its-Kind Auto Transport Service

Antonio Morado Jr | Program Manager, Guaranteed Pickup | Montway Auto Transport | Feb 2017 – Nov 2017
AT A GLANCE
35%reduction in inbound contact volume within 6 months
~300orders per month at peak season
$100k+profit in a single peak season
~10%of Montway's annual revenue today
4distributed team members managed across U.S. and internationally
Service launched from scratch in under 90 days with no existing playbook, no competitor benchmark, and no prior internal precedent.

THE SITUATION

Guaranteed Pickup was not a product when it was handed to me. It was an experiment, a loose concept being tested by one sales agent and one support agent with no defined process, no documented rules, and no framework for what success looked like. The sales agent would upsell a random amount depending on the order. The support agent would work to find a carrier and handle whatever came up. It worked occasionally, by accident.

The idea behind it was sound. Montway's Moving and Relocation team had been offering guaranteed pickup and delivery dates to B2B clients for years. The question was whether that model could be adapted for retail customers, who were a different audience entirely: higher volume, less predictable, and with no contracted relationship to fall back on when things went sideways.

I was asked to take it on eight months into my tenure at Montway after telling leadership I was ready to move to sales. They knew what they had in me and rather than let me leave the function, they offered me something harder and more interesting instead. I took it.

Two hours with the existing agents told me everything I needed to know. They were flying by the seat of their pants. There was no system, just improvisation. My job wasn't to refine what they were doing. It was to start over.

DISCOVERY BEFORE BUILDING

The first and most important decision I made was not to build anything immediately. Before I touched process design or pricing or tooling, I spent the first several weeks in meetings with every department that would be affected by or need to interact with Guaranteed Pickup.

I went to compliance and claims to understand the minimum requirements for carrier verification that would keep the service legally and operationally sound without creating unnecessary bottlenecks. I sat with the Moving and Relocation team to understand how their guaranteed service actually ran day-to-day, including its failure points. From them I also inherited a connection to their network of local tow and storage providers, which would become critical infrastructure for the service.

I worked with accounting to design the safest and most practical payment structure for both carriers and customers. The existing retail model used a deposit plus cash on delivery, which created risk and friction on both sides. We needed something cleaner for a service where trust and reliability were the entire value proposition.

In every one of those meetings I came in with the same framing: what do I need to do so that you can do what you do? I was explicit that I was there to take on the complexity, not add to it. Nobody likes being told they have new headaches. So I made sure that from their perspective, the new service would require nothing extra from them as long as I built my end correctly.

Every department I met wished me good luck on the way out. They could see what I was walking into. I took that as useful information and kept going.

BUILDING THE SYSTEM

Pricing and Payments

There was no existing pricing model for a guaranteed retail auto transport service. I built one from scratch using industry benchmarks, adding a surcharge designed to cover tow and storage costs when they were needed. The margin per order was solid but not predictable. The nature of the service meant that some orders would be smooth and some would require expensive interventions. Leadership's only requirement was that profits not dip below average retail order margins. I built that floor and engineered the surcharge to absorb the variance.

The payment structure required rethinking how we compensated carriers. The key insight was simple: if I could offer carriers more money than standard retail orders, many routes would effectively dispatch themselves. Better pay attracted better carriers, which meant fewer last-minute cancellations, fewer coverage gaps, and fewer situations where I needed to scramble for a backup. The economics of the service were designed around that dynamic from the start.

Rules, Constraints, and Booking Parameters

Before launch I documented the full operational rulebook for Guaranteed Pickup: pricing tiers, payment protocols, booking constraints including which locations and days of the week were serviceable and how far in advance orders needed to be placed, order notation requirements, order eligibility criteria, and a clear list of what the service would not accommodate. These weren't guidelines. They were hard parameters that every agent touching the service needed to understand and follow consistently.

The goal was to eliminate ambiguity at the point of sale. The single biggest failure mode in the original two-person experiment was that there were no rules, which meant every order was a judgment call. Judgment calls at scale produce inconsistency, and inconsistency in a guaranteed service destroys trust.

The Tow and Storage Network

The local tow and storage network I inherited from the relocation team was functional but it wasn't mine. The relationships had been built by other people, and the vendors had expectations calibrated to how the relocation team operated. My first task with the network was making clear that Guaranteed Pickup was running on different parameters and that I intended to be a reliable, consistent partner rather than an occasional caller with an emergency.

I maintained and updated the network map on a regular basis as new providers were added and existing relationships were validated. This was unglamorous work but it was load-bearing. When a carrier canceled at the last minute or a vehicle needed emergency storage, the quality of that network was the difference between a resolved situation and a customer crisis.

THE LAUNCH AND THE 80/20 MODEL

The service launched as a controlled rollout. Only a select group of sales agents could offer it initially, which kept volume manageable while the process was stress-tested. Live chat agents were added later once the core workflow had been proven and the documentation was solid enough to train from. The controlled rollout was a deliberate choice, not a limitation. Opening the service to full volume before the process was stable would have created a different kind of mess than the one I was trying to clean up.

In the early weeks I was extremely hands-on. I caught issues before they needed to be escalated, identified patterns in what was breaking, and refined the rules accordingly. The upfront discovery work with other departments paid off significantly here. Because I had already mapped the dependencies and negotiated the interfaces, most of the issues that surfaced were operational rather than structural.

The operational model I settled on was built around an 80/20 framework. Roughly 80% of orders would require only 20% of our effort. If the pricing was right and the order was posted with enough lead time, many carriers would book without intervention. The remaining 20% would require 80% of our attention: problem routes, last-minute cancellations, carrier no-shows, vehicle condition discrepancies. By designing the system to handle the easy 80% almost automatically, we freed up capacity to focus on the hard 20% that actually needed human attention.

The secret to scaling a guaranteed service isn't eliminating problems. It's building a system that handles routine orders so smoothly that you have the bandwidth to actually solve the hard ones.

THE TEAM

I requested two support agents and one dispatch agent from the start. The support agents were responsible for customer communication, explaining the process, setting expectations around potential tow and storage costs, and managing the customer relationship through to delivery. The dispatch agent handled orders that weren't moving smoothly, working the carrier side to find solutions when the standard process broke down.

Two of the team members were based internationally and worked on U.S. hours. They handled the dispatch function. The stateside team owned customer communication. That division was not arbitrary. American customers navigating a high-stakes logistics service carry specific expectations and communication norms that require cultural fluency to manage well. The international team was excellent at the operational, carrier-facing work. Putting them on customer communication would have created friction that wasn't necessary.

Managing a distributed team across time zones on a service with real-time operational demands required clear documentation, defined ownership at every step, and consistent communication rhythms. The same instinct that drove the departmental discovery process applied here: I needed my team to be able to do their jobs without needing to come to me for every decision. The rules and documentation existed so they could operate autonomously within a defined framework.

THE OUTCOMES

Within the first six months, inbound customer and carrier contact volume was running 35% lower than comparable retail orders. That number was the direct result of the proactive communication design. Customers who knew what to expect didn't call to ask, and carriers who were properly compensated didn't cancel without warning.

At steady state the service was processing 100 to 150 orders monthly at an average profit of $450 to $500 per order. Peak season pushed volumes to nearly 300 orders per month with total profit exceeding $100k in a single season.

Guaranteed Pickup now accounts for approximately 10% of Montway's annual revenue. For a company processing 250,000+ vehicle shipments annually at $130M+ in revenue, that is a material and durable contribution from a service that did not exist before February 2017.

That is one part of my lasting legacy at Montway, built entirely from a blank page.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

The most significant gap in how I ran Guaranteed Pickup was succession planning. I built a system that worked because I understood every part of it. The documentation was functional. Agents could follow the rules, but it wasn't deep enough to transfer the judgment behind the rules to someone who hadn't been in the room when they were made.

When I stepped back from the service, the person who took over began undoing processes almost immediately. Not because they were wrong to want changes, but because they didn't understand why the rules existed in the first place. Constraints that looked arbitrary from the outside were load-bearing. Within weeks, the departments I had carefully calibrated relationships with were feeling the friction again.

That's a documentation and knowledge transfer failure on my part. I built the plane and flew it well. I didn't write a manual thorough enough for someone else to fly it without me. In a product context, that's the difference between a system and a person-dependent workaround. I had built something closer to the latter than I wanted to admit at the time.

I would also have pushed harder and earlier for a formal product structure around the service: a roadmap, a defined set of success metrics, a regular review cadence with leadership. Guaranteed Pickup grew organically and operationally. It never had the strategic infrastructure that would have made it easier to defend, scale, or hand off. That was partly a resource constraint and partly a function of how the organization worked. But knowing what I know now, I would have fought for it.

Building something that works is the first job. Building something that works without you is the harder one. I got the first one right. The second one I'm still learning.

WHAT THIS CASE STUDY DEMONSTRATES

This is a product ownership story told in operations language. The skills required, including discovery before building, constraint mapping, stakeholder alignment, system design, distributed team management, controlled rollout, and iterative improvement, are the same skills that define effective product work in any context.

What made it possible wasn't a formal process or a defined methodology. It was a specific approach: go to the people affected before you build anything, take on the complexity so they don't have to, design for the common case and leave capacity for the hard ones, and stay close enough to the work to catch problems before they escalate.

Those instincts transfer. The industry changes. The tools change. The approach to building something that works in a constrained environment without a playbook is the same every time.
