Case Study: AI Enablement

Adopting AI Before the Memo: Three Systems Built Without a Playbook

Antonio Morado Jr | UX Specialist / Independent Builder | Montway Auto Transport & Self-Directed | 2024 – Present
AT A GLANCE
~10%reduction in paid keyword spend ahead of the AI Overviews disruption
0organic traffic decline post-AI Overviews, while the industry fell
120,000+customer reviews synthesized with AI, beyond keyword tagging
100-ptjob-scoring model: 7 weighted dimensions, 9 negative penalties
150+roles scored and triaged through a self-built local pipeline
None of these systems came with a playbook, a vendor, or a mandate. Each one started with a signal, a judgment call, and the willingness to be wrong in public.

THE SITUATION

In mid-2024, Google began rolling out AI Overviews and the search landscape that every marketing team had optimized for started moving under their feet. Most companies were reacting to headlines. At Montway, two people were positioned to read the shift as data rather than drama: the Sr. SEO Manager and me.

That positioning wasn’t luck. Well before the rollout, I had been attending webinars on AI’s evolving role in search and workflows, and both of us had been using AI tools daily for small tasks: unglamorous, practical use that built an honest sense of what the technology could and couldn’t do. When the shift arrived, we weren’t evaluating a rumor. We were watching something we’d been expecting.

The advantage wasn’t a tool or a budget. It was that we’d been paying attention for a year before it mattered.

THE SEO CALL: RESTRAINT AS STRATEGY

The signal showed up in the traffic data first. As AI Overviews rolled out, our paid traffic began to dip, but organic was holding strong and starting to rise. That divergence was the tell. AI Overviews were reshaping how search visibility worked, and they were not swayed by paid keyword spend.

Roughly a month after the rollout began, the Sr. SEO Manager and I brought the analysis to the rest of the team. The reception was receptive but uncertain. Most of the room had little understanding of what was actually happening, and the immediate assumption was that we were about to push for more AI-generated content. We were arguing the opposite. Montway’s existing content had already positioned the company to start appearing in AI Overviews. The winning move was to protect what was working, not flood it with generated material that could dilute exactly the authority the AI systems were rewarding.

My lane in the execution was deliberately narrow. The SEO and content teams knew their craft; they didn’t need me in it. What I provided was support and context to leadership: translating what the shift meant, why the traffic pattern looked the way it did, and what it implied for budget. The concrete recommendation I pushed: reduce paid keyword spend, because the surface customers were increasingly seeing was one that paid keywords couldn’t buy into. Spend came down by roughly 10%.

The outcome tracked the read. Paid traffic continued to shrink as the search landscape changed, as predicted, and now costing us 10% less to learn. Organic held steady, rising in some months, while others in the industry saw nothing but declines.

The recommendation that mattered most was to change almost nothing. When everyone around a new technology is selling motion, restraint backed by data is the harder call.

AI AS A DAILY OPERATING LAYER

The SEO call was one decision. The more durable work was quieter: making AI part of how I operated every day, in a company with no AI policy, no tooling budget for it, and no one asking me to.

Experimentation and Reporting

AI became my working partner for the A/B testing program: a sparring tool for formulating ideas and hypotheses before tests went to stakeholders, and an accelerant for the reporting that followed. It didn’t design the tests. It pressure-tested my thinking and cut the distance between a result and a readable report.

Reviews: From Keyword Counts to Meaning

The higher-stakes application was the review program. Before AI, our analysis of customer reviews ran on keyword tagging, and keywords never showed the true picture: a review mentioning “driver” could be praise, a complaint, or a fraud signal, and the tag couldn’t tell you which. Using AI to synthesize meaning rather than count words, I could finally show the business what its 120,000+ reviews were actually saying. The same capability produced response templates for positive reviews, giving a consistent brand voice to the side of reputation work that usually gets neglected.

Where It Failed, and the Workaround

AI was consistently weak at one thing: understanding our business. Auto transport brokerage is a niche industry with dynamics no model had meaningfully trained on, and outputs grounded in “our business” were generic or wrong. The workaround became a method: I stopped asking AI about our industry and started framing problems through analogies to industries it understood deeply, plus carefully built hypotheticals. The model supplied the reasoning; I supplied the translation back into our context. That division of labor, AI for the pattern and human for the business, is the single most transferable thing I learned from two years of daily use.

The people who get value from AI aren’t the ones who trust it most. They’re the ones who’ve mapped exactly where it breaks.

THE JOB-SEARCH SCORING SYSTEM

In 2026, I applied the same discipline to my own problem. A modern job search fails in a specific way: the volume is unmanageable, and fit judgment degrades as the search drags on: everything starts looking like a maybe. I built a system to hold the standard for me.

The Pipeline

A locally hosted app scans job posting sites for relevant title matches, pulls each role’s summary, and scores it. Postings that clear review move to a second-stage scoring artifact: same framework, but more nuanced and continuously updated as my search evolves. For roles worth pursuing, tailored resume and cover letter drafts go to a separate editing workflow that returns suggestions plus three alternative versions of the cover letter, so every submission is a choice among options rather than a first draft. I make the final edits and submit.

The Model

The scoring framework allocates 100 points across seven weighted dimensions: title match (30), keyword signal (25), seniority fit (15), location (12), compensation (8), company size (6), and posting recency (4). Score bands run from Strong Match down to Pass. Nine negative penalties catch the categories that look right and fit wrong: staffing firms, hardware, defense, heavy-sales roles disguised as CX. The penalties encode lessons that enthusiasm would otherwise ignore at 11pm on a Tuesday.

The model needed iteration to earn trust. The title dimension was the repeat offender: exact-match logic kept under-scoring roles whose titles didn’t match my target list but whose mandates did: the “first person to own customer feedback” jobs that hide under names like Experience Strategist or CX Lead. I adjusted the title scoring several times to capture adjacent titles, which is the entire thesis of my search made operational: the title is noise, the mandate is the match.

Validation Against Reality

With 150+ roles scored, the bands are showing predictive teeth. Roles scoring in the upper 60s to mid 70s have produced fast ATS rejections almost uniformly. Roles scoring above that range have produced a phone screen and a set of active, live follow-ups. A couple of postings have hit the 90+ range, and they got the deepest investment. That’s a small sample and I treat it as directional, not proven, but a scoring model whose failure band matches the market’s rejection pattern is doing its job: telling me where my time is worth spending.

The score doesn’t tell me where to apply. It tells me where my effort has a price worth paying. Those are different questions.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

At Montway, the honest gap is that AI adoption stayed a practitioner’s habit instead of becoming an organizational capability. The Sr. SEO Manager and I were the proactive users; everyone else got the benefits secondhand through our output. I never wrote the playbook: which prompts worked for review synthesis, how to frame problems the model couldn’t hold natively, where the failure lines were. When I left, that operating knowledge left with me. It’s the same lesson my Guaranteed Pickup retrospective surfaced: I build systems that work, and I under-invest in making them work without me. Naming a pattern twice means the third build carries the fix: documentation as part of the build, not an afterthought.

On the job-search system, I should have logged outcomes from the first application, not partway in. The band-to-outcome correlation I now find most valuable is built on incomplete early data. A system designed to fight motivated reasoning deserved rigorous record-keeping from day one.

WHAT THIS CASE STUDY DEMONSTRATES

AI enablement as a discipline is not about enthusiasm for tools. It is a repeating cycle: read the signal early and separate it from hype, translate the implications for decision-makers who don’t live in the details, operationalize without waiting for a playbook that doesn’t exist yet, map honestly where the technology fails, and validate against outcomes instead of impressions. I have run that full cycle three times: once on a company’s search strategy, once on its daily operations, and once on my own runway, where being wrong had a personal cost.

The industry positions change and the models change monthly. The cycle doesn’t. That’s the job, and this is what it looks like when I do it.
