Case Study: Reputation Rebuild

From Damaged to Deal-Maker: Rebuilding a Brand Reputation from 2.5 to 4.7 Stars

Antonio Morado Jr | Customer Experience Manager & Online Reputation Strategist | Montway Auto Transport | 2018–2022
AT A GLANCE
2.2star rating improvement (2.5 to 4.7)
120,000+reviews generated across platforms
20,000+Google reviews generated
77+NPS sustained
~98%reduction in cold-call conversion waste
Company acquired by AEA Investors in November 2020, with brand reputation cited as a primary driver.

THE SITUATION

Montway Auto Transport was built to be an order machine, not a customer service operation. The business model is straightforward: a customer needs a vehicle moved, Montway connects them with a carrier, the vehicle moves. For most retail customers, it's a one-and-done transaction. There's no loyalty program, no repeat purchase funnel, no relationship to nurture. You book, you wait, you get your car.

That model worked fine for scaling volume. It didn't work for building trust. By the time I moved into the Customer Experience Manager role in November 2018, Montway had a 2.5-star aggregate rating across review platforms, a score that reflected years of unmanaged customer feedback, a combative response style from leadership in public forums, and a claims team handling complaints with the primary objective of reinforcing that Montway, as a broker, carried no liability for anything that went wrong in transit.

There was no structured feedback infrastructure. No review strategy. No consistent voice in how the brand showed up publicly when things went wrong. What existed was a list of customers from the past 60 days and an instruction to call them and ask for a positive review.

The brand wasn't in crisis because of one bad event. It was in slow erosion from a thousand small failures that nobody had built a system to catch.

THE REAL PROBLEM

The stated problem was simple: we need better reviews. The actual problem was more layered, and getting to it required stepping back from the solution someone had already decided on before I arrived.

The cold-call review solicitation model had a 2% conversion rate. Of the customers who answered at all (most didn't), a meaningful portion wanted to complain rather than leave a positive review. The approach wasn't just ineffective. It was actively damaging. We were spending time and credibility on an outreach strategy that was retraumatizing customers with unresolved issues and producing almost nothing of value in return.

Underneath that was a deeper problem: the business had no mechanism for distinguishing between customers who had a good experience and customers who didn't before reaching out to ask for a review. Everyone got the same call. The assumption was that a delivered vehicle meant a satisfied customer. It didn't.

There was also an organizational culture problem. Leadership tracked metrics through phone activity. Calls made, calls answered, time on the phone. Moving away from cold calls meant moving away from the only measurement system they trusted. That wasn't a data problem. It was a change management problem disguised as one.

THE RESEARCH & METHODOLOGY

Diagnosing the Cold-Call Model

My collaborator and I began by pulling every data point we could connect to the existing outreach process. Outbound call logs, connection rates, conversion rates to actual review submissions, and customer satisfaction data from our post-delivery survey. The picture was clear: the juice wasn't worth the squeeze, and it never had been.

We then compared our cold-call conversion rate against published benchmarks for email outreach in service industries. Email alone, with no other changes, was projected to improve conversion by approximately 3 percentage points. That's not a minor efficiency gain on a 2% baseline. That's more than doubling output before optimizing anything else.

Designing the Gating System

The more important insight wasn't the channel. It was who we were contacting. Soliciting reviews from customers who had a bad experience doesn't produce positive reviews. It produces negative ones, and it produces them from people who were already going to leave them eventually. We needed to stop reaching out to the wrong people before we could start reaching out to the right ones.

Working with our internal data team, my collaborator pulled the operational signals we believed were most predictive of a poor customer experience. We identified six parameters that, when triggered, indicated the customer likely had friction in their order:

1. Supervisor request filed during the order lifecycle

2. Vehicle delivery delayed beyond standard window

3. Vehicle pickup outside of timely parameters

4. Order exceeded estimated timeframe by more than two days

5. Dispatching issues logged (carrier cancellations, acceptance failures)

6. Approval request triggered (vehicle condition discrepancy requiring additional payment)

We then cross-referenced that data against our post-delivery survey results. Customers who scored 8 or higher on a 1-10 satisfaction scale and had none of the above flags triggered became our outreach pool. Customers who scored below 8, or who had any of the six friction signals in their order, were excluded.

This wasn't a perfect science. We were working with the data we had access to, and Montway's systems weren't built for this kind of cross-referencing. But the logic was sound. We had independently arrived at what we later recognized as the promoter/passive/detractor framework from NPS methodology: contact the people most likely to say something positive, and leave everyone else alone until you've addressed their issue.

One of the clearest findings to emerge from this work wasn't about what went wrong. It was about what right looked like. Our best-performing orders, the ones most likely to produce a satisfied customer, were the ones where we never needed to speak to the customer past an initial verification call. Vehicle dispatched, picked up, delivered, without a single additional touchpoint. The moment our teams had to get involved for any reason, scores began to drop. That told us something important: the work had two parallel goals. The first was improving the quality of customer interactions when they were necessary. The second was reducing unnecessary touchpoints so there was less room for human error to enter the process. A smoother order wasn't just a happier customer. It was a more defensible one.

We reasoned our way to the same threshold that academic research on customer advocacy had already validated, not because we read about it, but because the question we were asking forced us there. Who would you ask for a review? Someone who loved the experience.

The Stakeholder Presentation

We brought the full analysis: call logs, conversion data, email benchmarks, the gating framework, and documentation maintained in Google Sheets, to the Claims and Compliance manager first. She was data-literate and understood the argument quickly. That meeting went well.

The VP and President were a different conversation. Their hesitation wasn't about the data. It was about control. Phone calls were measurable in the ways they trusted. Email felt like giving something up. We anticipated this and prepared a secondary argument: moving off cold calls would free us to do something they cared about even more: responding to the negative reviews that were actively suppressing the rating. That landed. The combination of efficiency gain and reputation defense gave them a path to yes, and they took it.

Cold calls for review solicitation were eliminated company-wide. The email system launched in approximately April or May of 2019.

THE CONSTRAINTS

The reputation rebuild didn't happen in a clean environment. It happened despite a set of organizational constraints that we never fully resolved.

We were locked out of B2B entirely. Montway's corporate relocation and OEM logistics customers were off limits for feedback solicitation. Too many layers of contracted relationships between us and the actual end user. We couldn't audit live chat or QA agents. We couldn't get anywhere near the sales team, which was the source of many downstream problems: orders booked without proper expectation setting, incorrect or incomplete information entered into the system, approvals triggered because a carrier showed up to find a vehicle in different condition than described.

By the time a retail customer reached dispatch or support, there were often already problems baked into the order that nobody in the post-sale process had the authority or access to prevent. We built workarounds. We worked directly with supervisors, updated email templates to improve clarity, created SOPs for handling common friction scenarios, and tried to give agents better tools within the lanes they were allowed to operate in.

We built and maintained a review response practice, responding to both positive and negative reviews across platforms. What we couldn't do was the proactive community engagement we knew would amplify the work: participating in Reddit threads, Facebook groups, industry forums where auto transport was being discussed. We wanted to get ahead of conversations before they became complaints. We were always reactive when we wanted to be proactive, and the difference between those two modes is significant when you're trying to build genuine brand trust rather than just manage damage.

The deflections we heard most often when pushing for deeper access were consistent: 'Our competition does it this way,' though we had no meaningful competition at scale. 'We'll lose order conversions,' without data to support it. The business had optimized for volume over quality for so long that even evidence-backed arguments for change were treated with suspicion.

We weren't fixing the system. We were building a workaround that performed well enough to move the numbers despite the system. That distinction matters when you're assessing what the outcome actually represents.

THE YELP CHAPTER

Yelp deserves its own section because it consumed significant energy and ultimately taught us something valuable about when to stop fighting.

Montway's raw review average on Yelp sat around 4.2. Yelp's proprietary recommendation algorithm, which they describe as providing a 'more realistic view' of a business, suppressed that to somewhere between 2.5 and 3.2, a number that fluctuated month to month based on criteria they never fully disclosed. No amount of positive review generation moved it meaningfully because the algorithm filtered out the reviews we were generating.

We did everything we could within Yelp's guidelines to improve the standing. It didn't move. Upper management treated it as a constant failure, which was frustrating given that the Yelp number was structurally outside our control.

The situation clarified itself in 2021 or 2022 when Yelp approached Montway with a proposal: pay approximately $50,000 to maintain placement in their top auto transport listings, justified by the traffic they claimed to be sending us. We requested a full breakdown of their traffic data so we could compare it against our own analytics. When we ran the comparison, it was unambiguous. Montway had driven more traffic to Yelp in six months than Yelp had driven to Montway in four years.

We declined. And we finally had the data to convince upper management to stop treating Yelp as a meaningful metric. The decision to deprioritize Yelp and concentrate our energy on Google and other platforms where our reviews could actually land was overdue, and the traffic analysis closed the argument.

Sometimes the most important strategic decision is knowing which battle to stop fighting. Yelp wasn't a reputation problem we failed to solve. It was a platform incentive problem we eventually proved and walked away from.

THE OUTCOMES

The score trajectory wasn't linear and I want to be precise about what I can state with confidence versus what I'm estimating from memory, as I no longer have access to Montway's internal data.

What I know: we started at 2.5 in November 2018. We were in the low 4.0 range by January 2020, approximately 14 months of work. By the time my title changed to Online Reputation Strategist in February 2021, we were approaching the low 4.2-4.3 range. The 4.7 rating landed somewhere between late 2021 and mid 2022. The improvement in the later phase was partly a function of scale. More reviews meant more stability in the aggregate, and partly a function of the platform shift away from Yelp.

Across the full program, we generated 120,000+ reviews including 20,000+ on Google. NPS was sustained above 77 throughout.

In November 2020, Montway was acquired by AEA Investors. The company's improved brand reputation and online presence were cited as a primary driver of the deal. I was not in that room, and I can't quote the acquisition documents. What I know is what leadership communicated internally, and what became part of the company's public narrative around the transaction.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

The honest answer to this question is that most of what I would do differently wasn't within my control at the time. The access restrictions, the resource constraints, the organizational resistance to QA and sales integration. Those were real walls. But there are things I could have done better within the lanes I had.

We had a review response strategy and we executed it consistently, responding to both positive and negative reviews across platforms. What we couldn't do was the proactive outreach we knew would move the needle further. Engaging on Reddit threads, participating in Facebook groups where auto transport came up, getting ahead of conversations before they became complaints. We wanted to be proactive, not just reactive. The bandwidth was never there. Given what we now know about how active community engagement correlates with brand trust and organic review generation, that gap had a real cost.

I would have documented the methodology more formally from the start. We kept Google Sheets logs and we built the process as we went, but we didn't produce anything that looked like a research readout. The work was credible. The data backed every decision, but the paper trail was functional rather than strategic. In a larger organization, that documentation becomes the foundation for scaling the function. We built the plane while flying it, which was necessary, but I would have been more intentional about leaving a record.

And I would have fought harder for even one additional person on the team. Two people running a reputation function for a company processing 250,000+ vehicle shipments annually, while also handling escalations, writing SOPs, redrafting company communications, and managing the politics of half a dozen departments was not sustainable. We knew it. We said it. We never got the resource. The ceiling on what we could accomplish was always set by what two people could physically do.

The outcome was real. But the outcome we could have produced with proper resourcing would have been different. That's not a complaint. It's the honest answer to the question.

WHAT THIS CASE STUDY DEMONSTRATES

This case study isn't primarily a story about reputation management. It's a story about consumer insights applied under constraints.

The work required diagnosing the real problem before accepting the stated one, designing a research methodology from available signals rather than ideal inputs, building a stakeholder argument grounded in data and framed for the audience in the room, executing against organizational resistance without the authority to mandate change, and knowing when to stop fighting a battle that the data proved was unwinnable.

Those are the skills that transfer. The platform changes. The industry changes. The instinct for finding the real signal in the noise, staying in the room until it becomes a decision, doesn't.
