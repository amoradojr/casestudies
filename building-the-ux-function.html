Case Study: Building the UX Function

A Function of One: Building UX, Experimentation, and Accessibility Across Four Brands

Antonio Morado Jr | UX Specialist | Montway Auto Transport | Jan 2024 – Nov 2025

A UX function built from a pitch, not a mandate: no playbook, no headcount, and no precedent in a company that had never had one.

THE SITUATION

Before 2020, website decisions at Montway were made by the CEO and the general manager. Design authority lived at the top, evidence was informal, and the site showed it. In 2020 the company hired a VP of Marketing, and in 2021 a coworker and I were assigned to marketing to help build the team out. That was the opening: for the first time, there was an organization that decisions could be argued into rather than handed down from.

By the time the UX role was created in 2024, I had already been running A/B tests in my Online Reputation role, and the company was acquiring brands faster than anyone was coordinating their web experiences. Design decisions ran through our Creative Director, and he consulted me before moving forward. The function existed informally before it existed officially: I was already the person people checked with. So I pitched making it a job. As the properties multiplied, someone needed to be the point person, and I had been acting as one without the title.

The pitch wasn’t for a team or a department. It was for permission to formalize work I was already doing, across brands that were already colliding.

BUYING THE RIGHT CONSTRAINT: THE TESTING PLATFORM

Our first experimentation ran on Optimizely, and the constraint wasn’t skill, it was the plan: two to three tests a month against a backlog that deserved far more, and gaps in what we could test at all. A testing program that can’t keep pace with its own hypothesis list isn’t a program. It’s a queue.

In 2022 we evaluated alternatives. The deciding factor was architectural: our properties ran on React, and AB Tasty was one of the only platforms that could genuinely support testing on it. When the evaluation finished, the winner was clear, and the business case I helped carry made AB Tasty the company-wide platform. The broader tooling followed the same pattern: I scoped and justified the stack, including HotJar, AccessiBe, and research tooling, into a $150k budget envelope. I didn’t control the allocation. I made the case for what belonged inside it, and leadership funded it.

THE EXPERIMENTATION PROGRAM

Over roughly two years I ran about 30 A/B tests across conversion flows, page layouts, button treatments, and checkout content for an 18% cumulative lift in quote completions. That metric choice was deliberate honesty: attribution to completed orders didn’t exist yet in our analytics, so I measured what the data could actually support rather than claiming what it couldn’t.

The number I watched as closely as the lift was the approval rate. Roughly 90% of what I brought to stakeholders, both test proposals and the operational and process changes that came out of results, was approved. For an individual contributor with no direct reports, that rate is the whole game. Influence in that seat isn’t granted by an org chart. It’s earned by being right, repeatedly, in front of people who remember your last recommendation.

Individual test designs, hypotheses, and results are Montway proprietary information and are described here only in aggregate.
An IC changes how a company makes decisions one approved recommendation at a time. The 18% mattered. The 90% is what made the next 18% possible.

ACCESSIBILITY: FROM IGNORED TO IMPLEMENTED

I had been vocal about accessibility since my Online Reputation days, when our site still looked like a relic of the early 2010s, scrolling ticker and all. For years that advocacy went nowhere. What changed wasn’t my argument. It was the messenger: one of our outside legal counsel contractors acknowledged the site was a genuine legal risk, and suddenly the VP of Marketing and legal were working the problem.

While they evaluated solutions, I did the part I could control: I had an audit run to map exactly where we stood. It surfaced 144 accessibility errors, including 19 major violations spanning color contrast, missing alt text, and screen reader navigation, with alt text making up the majority. The company chose AccessiBe as the remediation path, and I led the implementation across the flagship site and two of its brands, finding the right widget placement page by page and adjusting until it worked with each design.

I’ll be direct about the trade-off, because informed people know it: an overlay provides compliance coverage by compensating for issues in the rendered experience, but the underlying errors stay in the codebase. Most of ours were alt-text gaps that a content effort could have fixed at the source. The implementation was real, the coverage was real, and the risk came down. The remediation I actually wanted was deeper, and that fight is in the next section.

Four years of being right about accessibility moved nothing. One sentence from outside counsel moved everything. The lesson isn’t cynicism. It’s knowing which messenger a message needs.

QA: THE ROLE NOBODY ASKED FOR

During our first UAT ahead of a launch, it became clear the review wasn’t going to hold: my peers were either overworked or not technical enough to describe what they were seeing in a way developers could act on. So I started auditing the pages that had been missed and the issues that didn’t make sense, which turned into going over everything. Nobody assigned that. It needed to happen, so I did it. Afterward, the role was formally mine, and I served as QA lead across four website launches and a full site migration.

The midnight launch is the honest picture of what that role cost. I spent the working day confirming every requested change had been properly logged and submitted, logged in an hour before launch to walk the game plan with our third-party team and the marketing staff on live audit, and then we went live into a mess: images and pages not loading, dead links, 404 errors. I stayed on until 4am reporting everything I could find, logged back in for my normal shift, and worked through into the next day. Twenty-five hours on the clock because catching it in staging and the first hours live is cheap, and catching it from customer complaints is not.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

First, I would have pushed for backend remediation earlier and armed with more data. The spaghetti code running through the site and into our analytics taxed everything: the GA4 migration cost me months of troubleshooting why testing data wasn’t coming through, and simple changes like an H1 or a button lived in places no one would reasonably look. I’m not a developer, but I can work my way around HTML, and it consistently took too long to find what I needed because it lived somewhere it shouldn’t. I treated that as friction to route around. It was actually a quantifiable cost, and I had the data to price it out and force the conversation. I never did.

Second, the alt-text debt. I would have fought harder for source-level fixes alongside the overlay, starting with the alt-text majority that a focused content sprint could have retired. Coverage and remediation are different things, and I knew it at the time.

Third, the ending. I was laid off in November 2025, and as far as I’m aware the UX function dissolved with my departure. No one was brought in to take it over. My Guaranteed Pickup retrospective taught me that documentation is what lets a system survive its builder. This taught me the harder half of that lesson: institutionalization isn’t only the builder’s job. A function of one survives on organizational will, and I never secured the commitment that would have made the function permanent instead of personal. The next time I build a function, the succession case gets made at the start, when leverage is highest, not at the end.

Guaranteed Pickup outlived me because the system didn’t need me. The UX function didn’t outlive me because the organization never decided it needed the function, only the person. Those are different failures, and only one of them was fully mine.

WHAT THIS CASE STUDY DEMONSTRATES

This is an influence story. Every durable change in it, the testing platform, the experimentation practice, the accessibility implementation, the QA function, was driven by an individual contributor with no direct reports, operating on a mandate he had to construct himself. The mechanics are repeatable: earn the informal role before pitching the formal one, measure honestly even when a bigger claim is available, know which messenger a message needs, and treat stakeholder trust as the asset that compounds.

The gaps are named as plainly as the wins, because the pattern across my case studies is the point: I build functions from nothing, and each build has taught me more about what it takes to make them permanent. That learning curve is the product.
